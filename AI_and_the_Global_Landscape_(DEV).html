One of the key challenge facing society today is in ensuring Artificial Intelligence (AI) doesn’t bring negative or even harmful risks for individuals. 
Preventing such negative repercussions is the reason, since 2016, a number of national and international organizations as well as private sector companies have ben working on standards and guidance documents.

These publications are intended to promote the responsible and ethical use of AI while mitigating its potential risks, thereby ensuring that AI can be developed and used for the benefit of society as a whole.
As Public Servants, we have a duty to protect Public trust: The use of AI has the potential to affect the lives of Canadian in significant ways. As such, we have a responsibility to ensure that the use of AI is transparent, ethical, and in the public interest. By being aware of the latest developments in AI regulation, research, and development, we can secure public trust when we using AI.

This online self-paced course will take you on a journey through AI global governance and the most important AI guidelines and decisions. It will bring to focus the AI Act: proposed regulation by the European Union that aims to establish a legal framework for the development, deployment, and use of artificial intelligence systems.

Governance can be defined as “The system by which entities are directed and controlled. It is concerned with:

  • structure and processes for decision making,
  • accountability,
  • control and
  • behaviour at the top of an entity.

But why are we talking of global governance?

“Global governance involves decisions that transcend boundaries. The resolutions are intended for the international, transnational, and regional levels. These decisions are based on rights and rules that are enforced through a combination of economic and moral incentives. 
There are many examples where global governance has been used in the past to manage issues that cut across national borders:
•    a war,
•    a pandemic
•    climate change
•    geo-economic dispute.

Global governance aims to provide global public goods, particularly peace and security, justice and mediation systems for conflict, functioning markets and unified standards for trade and industry.”

Module 3: AI Governance in the world today

While AI legislation is still in its early stages, there is growing recognition of the need for such legislation to ensure that AI is developed and used in a responsible and ethical manner. 

These efforts have happened both in the public sector and on National and international organizations who have responded to the concerns posed by the twin objective of promoting the uptake of AI and of addressing the risks associated with certain uses of such technology.

Public Servants should stay informed about the EU AI Act: 
The EU AI Act is a proposed regulation that aims to set standards for the development and use of artificial intelligence within the European Union. It includes guidelines on ethical and transparent AI, as well as requirements for high-risk AI systems.

Here are a few ways in which the EU AI Act may impact global standards for AI:
Setting a precedent for regulation: The EU AI Act is one of the first major pieces of legislation specifically focused on AI regulation.

Other countries may look to the EU's approach and use it as a model for their own AI regulations. Encouraging international cooperation: The EU AI Act emphasizes the importance of international cooperation on AI regulation and governance. The Act includes several specifications related to the scrutiny of AI systems. These are categorized into 4 levels of scrutiny based on the potential risk of the AI system.

To ensure that the AI systems they develop and deploy are trustworthy and aligned with the public interest, public servants should keep these levels top of mind

    
1-    Prohibited    Systems that manipulate human behavior, social credit scoring, and systems that use subliminal techniques to exploit vulnerabilities in human decision-making. 

Prohibited AI systems: These are AI systems that are considered high-risk and are prohibited under the AI Act.

2 - Strict    Systems include those used in critical infrastructure, such as transportation and healthcare.

Strict AI systems: These are AI systems that are considered high-risk and are subject to strict regulatory oversight.     

3 - Limited    Examples of limited AI systems include those used in customer service and chatbots.

Limited AI systems: These are AI systems that are considered medium-risk and are subject to less stringent regulatory oversight.    

4 - Minimal    Examples of minimal AI systems include those used in video games and personal assistants.

Minimal AI systems: These are AI systems that are considered low-risk and are subject to minimal regulatory oversight.
